Clothes dryer electricity cost in 2026: electric vs gas vs heat pump.

Bruno de Madrazo
Updated July 16, 2026·How we calculate this

The clothes dryer is the third-largest residential electricity load after HVAC and water heating — typically 5-8% of an annual bill. The difference between the cheapest and most expensive way to dry clothes is roughly $100/yr. Here are the four real choices and when each pays back.

TL;DR: At US-average rates, 260 cycles/yr: electric resistance dryer = $138/yr, gas dryer = $85/yr, heat pump dryer = $45/yr. Hanging clothes outside = $0. Heat pump dryer is the cleanest electric upgrade if you've already cut gas elsewhere.

What a dryer costs by state

Every number above uses the 2026 US-average rate of $0.175/kWh — but almost nobody pays the average. EIA residential rates in 2026 run from 12.35¢/kWh in North Dakota to 46.62¢/kWh in Hawaii, a 3.8× spread. Same dryer, same 260 loads of laundry, wildly different bill. The formula is the one this whole guide runs on: annual cost = 260 cycles × kWh per cycle × your state rate — 3.0 kWh/cycle for electric resistance (780 kWh/yr), 1.1 kWh/cycle for heat pump (286 kWh/yr). Why heat pump dryers will eat this category

A heat pump dryer recirculates the same warm air, using a heat exchanger to wring out moisture rather than venting hot air outdoors. Two consequences:

  • 50-60% less electricity than resistance: the heat is moved, not generated.
  • No exterior vent required: install anywhere with a 240V outlet (or 120V for compact models). Game-changer for apartments and second floors.
  • Runs cooler and longer: ~90 minutes per load at 110-130 °F vs ~50 minutes at 150-170 °F for resistance. Gentler on clothes (less shrinkage, less pilling).
  • More expensive upfront: $1,200-$1,800 vs $700-$1,100 for resistance.

Payback math: $138 − $45 = $93/yr saved. On a $500 premium ($1,500 heat pump vs $1,000 resistance, midpoints), that's about a 5.4-year payback on electricity alone. If you're replacing a 12-year-old broken resistance dryer anyway, the premium is the only number that matters and the math is essentially a "free upgrade".

Ways to cut dryer cost without replacing

  1. Clean the lint filter every load + the duct annually. Lint-clogged ducts add 15-30% to drying time.
  2. Use moisture sensing, not timed cycles. 95% of modern dryers sense — turn it on if you've been using timed.
  3. Skip dryer balls and "wrinkle release" settings. Marketing line items; energy waste.
  4. Air-dry heavier items (jeans, towels) and finish in dryer for 10 min. Cuts dryer time by 40%.
  5. Avoid running with 2 items. The drum heats regardless of load — fill it 75% full.

Five mistakes people make when running the dryer math

  1. Using the national average instead of your rate. $0.175/kWh describes almost nobody. At 12.35¢ (North Dakota) the resistance-to-heat-pump saving is $61/yr; at 46.62¢ (Hawaii) it's $230/yr. Every payback figure in this guide moves proportionally with your rate — the spread turns a 2-year payback into an 8-year one.
  2. Comparing the dollar figures on EnergyGuide labels. The yellow label's "$/yr" is computed at one assumed national rate. Ignore the dollars; take the label's kWh/yr and multiply by your own rate from your bill (total charges ÷ kWh used — that captures delivery fees the advertised supply rate hides).
  3. Assuming a longer cycle means more energy. A heat pump dryer runs ~90 minutes against ~50 for resistance, yet uses roughly a third of the electricity per load — 1.1 kWh vs 3.0 kWh. Energy is power × time, and the heat pump draws far less power for those extra minutes. Judge kWh per load, never minutes.
  4. Buying a plain condenser dryer thinking it's a heat pump. Both are ventless, and retailers blur them. A compact condenser without a heat pump uses ~2.4 kWh/cycle — more than double a heat pump's 1.0-1.2 kWh, and $109/yr vs $45-$55 at average rates. If the spec sheet doesn't say "heat pump", it isn't one.
  5. Doing the payback on the full price instead of the premium. If your dryer is dead, you're spending $1,000 on a resistance unit regardless. The only number the heat pump has to justify is the ~$500 difference — which the Massachusetts example above clears in 3.4 years, not the 10+ years you get by dividing $1,500 by the saving.

Is a heat pump dryer worth it for you?

Go heat pump if…

  • Your old dryer just died — you compare only the ~$500 premium, not the full price.
  • You run 5+ loads a week, so the ~$86/yr saving actually adds up.
  • You have no exterior vent (apartment, second floor, interior laundry) — ventless installs anywhere with an outlet.
  • You're electrifying the whole home and want to drop the gas line.

Stick with electric resistance (or gas) if…

  • Your current dryer still works — replacing it means paying the full ~$1,500 price to save $86/yr, a ~17-year payback. The 5-6 year math only applies to the ~$500 premium when you're buying a dryer anyway.
  • You dry only a couple of loads a week — the saving shrinks below ~$40/yr.
  • You want the fastest cycle — heat pump runs ~90 min vs ~50 min for resistance.
  • You already have cheap gas and a vented gas dryer at $86/yr — close to heat pump running cost without the upfront jump.

Frequently asked questions

How much does it cost to run a clothes dryer?

Electric resistance: $138/yr at US-average rates (260 cycles). Gas: $85. Heat pump: $45-$55.

Are heat pump dryers worth the higher price?

5-6 year payback on electricity alone if you'd be buying a dryer anyway. Worth it for new construction or whole-home electrification.

Is a clothesline really $140/yr cheaper than a dryer?

Yes for an electric household. The trade-off is the labor of hanging + taking down.

Which states pay the most to run a clothes dryer?

Hawaii, at 46.62¢/kWh (EIA residential rate), pays about $364/yr for a resistance dryer at 260 cycles — vs $96/yr in North Dakota at 12.35¢. Same machine, 3.8× difference. California ($275), Connecticut ($251), Massachusetts and New York ($230) follow.

How do I calculate my dryer's cost per load?

kWh per cycle × your rate. A resistance dryer uses ~3.0 kWh/cycle: $0.37/load at North Dakota's 12.35¢/kWh, $1.40 at Hawaii's 46.62¢. Get your true rate by dividing your bill's total charges by the kWh consumed.

Does a heat pump dryer pay back faster in expensive-electricity states?

Yes — the switch saves ~494 kWh/yr at 260 cycles. Hawaii: $230/yr, ~2.2-year payback on the $500 premium. Massachusetts: $145/yr, 3.4 years. Idaho: $63/yr, ~8 years — longer than many people keep a dryer.
